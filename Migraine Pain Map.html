Migraine Pain Map

A structured description of a consistent, recurring pain pattern — prepared as a reference to bring to a physiotherapy, GP, or neurology appointment.

This document records one person’s self-observed pain pattern and a plain-language mapping of which anatomical structures those sensations may correspond to. It is a starting point for discussion with a qualified clinician — not a diagnosis. The reproducibility of the pattern (the same locations every time) is the key feature worth highlighting to whoever assesses it.

1. The Pain Map — Sensation to Structure

Dense, knotty bundle of tightness
Just above the collarbone, where the neck meets the shoulder

Most consistent with the scalenes and the junction of the levator scapulae / upper trapezius. The scalenes are a recognised migraine trigger-point site and sit exactly in this region; they lie close to the brachial plexus and nearby blood vessels.

Thick muscle, painful peak
Behind the ear

Likely the splenius capitis and the upper attachment of the sternocleidomastoid (SCM) at the mastoid process. The SCM is well known for referring pain behind the eyes, into the temple, and into the teeth — which matches the facial symptoms below.

Two ridge-like muscles, pain intensifying at the “knobbly bits”
Back of the neck, up to the base of the skull

The ridges correspond to splenius and semispinalis; the knobbly attachment points are the suboccipital muscles at the skull base. This is the classic cervicogenic headache region. These small muscles have a direct fascial connection to the dura mater (the brain’s covering), one proposed route by which neck tension becomes head pain.

Pain following the head round; very bad behind the eyes; into teeth and jaw
Front of head / face

The combined referral zone of the SCM and suboccipital muscles, with likely trigeminal nerve involvement (it supplies the face, teeth, and the lining around the brain). See the mechanism note below.

Strong pulse and the visible outline of a vein
Just below the temple, next to the ear, on the affected side

Most likely the superficial temporal artery. Visible pulsation and throbbing here during an attack is a recognised vascular feature of migraine, layered on top of the muscular pattern.

Clustered pain points along the spine (same locations each time)
Lower back and mid-back

Base of the back, between spine and pelvis: the quadratus lumborum (QL) and the SI joint / iliolumbar region.
Midway up, beside the spine: the erector spinae / thoracic paraspinals.
Their consistency suggests a whole-spine postural pattern rather than isolated local pain.

2. The Likely Connecting Mechanism

The upper neck nerves (C1–C3) and the trigeminal nerve converge on the same region in the brainstem — the trigeminocervical complex. This convergence is the leading explanation for why a neck/muscular problem can produce front-of-head and facial migraine symptoms (behind the eyes, throbbing temple, jaw and teeth).

In plain terms: tension and irritation in the neck muscles can feed into — or amplify — the migraine system, while the throbbing temple artery adds the vascular layer on top. The whole-spine cluster pattern suggests a postural chain running the length of the back, potentially the same asymmetry felt in the shoulder.

Why this is somewhat hopeful: the muscular and postural contributors are modifiable, in a way that pure migraine sometimes is not. Treatment targeting the cervical component reduces migraine frequency for some people.

3. Additional Observations Worth Mentioning

  • Visible muscular asymmetry: rolling the right shoulder forward makes a distinct cord-like muscle (likely levator scapulae) pop up; the same motion on the left shows nothing. Suggests the right side is hypertrophied or chronically contracted.
  • Pattern is reproducible: the same pain locations recur with every attack — a feature that points toward a structural/myofascial contribution rather than random activity.
  • Pain consistently involves the neck, and usually the lower back too — pointing to a full postural chain rather than a purely local issue.

4. Suggested Next Steps

  • Physiotherapist specialising in headache / cervicogenic pain — to assess the asymmetry, the upper cervical spine, and the postural chain, and to guide safe loading and release work.
  • GP or neurologist — particularly if the migraines are not already being formally managed. The same-every-time mapping plus the vascular features (throbbing, visible temporal artery) are worth them knowing.
One caution
Because this involves the upper cervical spine, and a pulsating artery can be felt at the temple, avoid aggressive self-manipulation and do not let anyone forcefully “crack” the neck without a proper assessment first. Gentle, guided work is appropriate; force in this region is not.
Prepared as a personal reference for clinical discussion. Not a diagnosis. Anatomical mappings are general and approximate — a hands-on assessment is needed to confirm what is actually driving the pattern.
