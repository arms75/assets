Study · 12-week plan

SQL self-study syllabus

Foundations of SQL for data analysis — joins, aggregation, subqueries and data manipulation, finishing with a small portfolio project on real or sample data.

Duration
12 weeks
Weekly
~2.5 hrs — Sat 11:15–12:45 + optional Sun 10:00–11:00
Goal
Working proficiency, ending with a documented multi-table query you can show your team or talk about in an interview.
Setup resources & rhythm
The four resources
  • Book — Sams Teach Yourself SQL in 10 Minutes (Ben Forta, 22 lessons). Primary reading & concept reference.
  • SQLBolt — interactive lessons at sqlbolt.com. Hands-on practice in the browser.
  • Video — one free video course (picks below). Reinforce each topic visually.
  • Sandbox — SQLite + a sample DB (e.g. Chinook) or your SIS read-access. Apply skills to realistic data.
Video picks — choose one, sample first
  • TechTFQ — topic-by-topic, clear logical explanations, includes a PostgreSQL setup guide. Best fit for this structure.
  • Alex the Analyst — short ~10-min videos per topic, analyst-focused.
  • freeCodeCamp — comprehensive long-form single-video courses; good as a jump-around reference.
  • Data with Baraa — extensive long-form playlists, free version of paid content.

Match one focused video to the topic you are reading that week rather than a 20+ hour marathon. Sample two channels in week 1 and lock in whichever clicks.

How each week works
  • Read the assigned book lesson(s) — short, ~10 min each.
  • Watch a video segment on the same topic.
  • Practice the matching SQLBolt lesson(s).
  • Apply — write 2–3 of your own queries against the sample DB (or your SIS data).
  • Log one useful query to your running personal cheat-sheet.

The optional Sunday hour is a buffer / catch-up slot — use it only if you fell behind. Not mandatory.

After week 12 where next
  • Window functions (ROW_NUMBER, RANK, running totals) — the natural intermediate next step; high-value for analyst work.
  • Python + pandas — the planned follow-on; pandas will feel familiar after SQL.
  • Keep the cheat-sheet alive — your running list of useful queries is the most valuable artifact you build here.
