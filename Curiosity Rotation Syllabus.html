Block A — The History of Life on Earth ~8 weeks

Your dinosaurs / deep-time / historical-animals umbrella — life on Earth as a single narrative, shaped by a constantly changing planet. (Formal names: paleobiology, historical biogeography, deep time.)

Backbone — structured course
  • Dino 101: Dinosaur Paleobiology (Coursera, University of Alberta) — free, 12 lessons, beginner. Museum & dig-site video lectures with quizzes; its set textbook is Brusatte’s Dinosaur Paleobiology, so your reading feeds straight in.
  • Follow-ons (same university, if you continue): Ancient Marine Reptiles; Theropod Dinosaurs & the Origin of Birds; Early Vertebrate Evolution.
Reading spine
  • The Rise and Fall of the Dinosaurs — Steve Brusatte. Narrative, beginner-friendly; pairs with Dino 101.
  • A Short History of Nearly Everything — Bill Bryson. The deep-time, shifting-continents, “humans are a blip” wonder.
  • The Rise and Reign of the Mammals — Steve Brusatte. Continues the story past the dinosaurs.
Watch — supplementary
  • PBS Eons (YouTube) — standalone ~10-min episodes; pick ones matching your topic. A fan “Deep Time Playlist” orders them.
  • Prehistoric Planet (Apple TV+) — for the visceral “what was it actually like” hit.
Suggested arc — video → written → applied
  • Wks 1–2: Dino 101 lessons 1–3 + matching Brusatte chapters; lesson quizzes as your applied step.
  • Wks 3–4: Dino 101 lessons 4–7; Eons episodes on extinctions.
  • Wks 5–6: Dino 101 lessons 8–12; Bryson deep-time chapters (geology, plate tectonics).
  • Wks 7–8: Begin Rise & Reign of the Mammals; pick an essay thread.
Block goalA confident mental timeline of life on Earth, and a felt sense of how a changing planet drove what lived, thrived, and vanished.
Block B — Conceptual Physics & Space ~8 weeks

Understanding-first, maths-light. Genuinely grasp the big ideas (motion, energy, entropy, relativity, cosmology) conceptually. Maths is optional, added later only if a specific concept makes you want it.

Backbone — structured course
  • Khan Academy — Physics — free, structured units; short videos + written articles + interactive practice, your whole loop in one place.
  • Start with Middle School Physics (Motion & Forces, Energy, Waves), then step up to Physics 1. For space: the Cosmology & Astronomy unit.
Reading spine
  • Conceptual Physics — Paul Hewitt. The maths-light textbook standard; your written reference.
  • Cosmos — Carl Sagan. Awe plus understanding; pair with the original series.
  • Astrophysics for People in a Hurry — Neil deGrasse Tyson. Short, accessible space primer.
Watch — supplementary
  • PBS Space Time — the “Curved Spacetime in General Relativity” playlist (12 videos). Early episodes build intuition with no maths; later ones get rigorous — slow down rather than push.
  • Veritasium & Sixty Symbols (YouTube) — single-concept deep dives for the “go deeper” moments.
Suggested arc — video → written → applied
  • Wks 1–2: Khan MS Physics — Motion & Forces + matching Hewitt chapters.
  • Wks 3–4: Khan — Energy; entropy concepts; Veritasium for intuition.
  • Wks 5–6: Relativity conceptually — PBS Space Time relativity playlist (no maths required).
  • Wks 7–8: Khan Cosmology & Astronomy; Sagan / Tyson; pick an essay thread.
Block goalComfortably follow and discuss big physics ideas conceptually — and know which one you’d want to unlock with maths, if any.

Note: the Feynman Lectures are a maths-heavy undergraduate course in disguise — a destination, not a start. Save as an “if I get hooked” reward.

Blender — Weekly Making-Pursuit ongoing

Sat 18:00–19:30, every week. A continuous project track, NOT a rotation subject — last week’s progress carries into this week. Builds 3D + creative confidence now, and feeds game dev later if that pull strengthens.

Backbone — structured course
  • CG Fast Track — Blender Track (you own this). You are here: Rendering in Cycles (Intermediate).
  • Next up the track: Compositing → Hard Surface Boot Camp Vols 1–3 → Procedural Shaders → Organic Modeling → Sculpting → Rigging → Geometry Nodes.
Written companion
  • Blender official docs (docs.blender.org) — free, current, organised like CG Fast Track. Read the matching section after each module.
  • The Complete Guide to Blender Graphics — John M. Blain. A book-shaped reference for later.

Per session: watch the CG Fast Track segment → read the matching docs section → apply it in your own file. Keep it weekly — continuity beats hours; skipping weeks is what kills slow Blender learning.

Essay writing loose practice

Target ~one short essay (500–1000 words) per rotation block, tied to what you’ve been reading. The reading is the fuel; the essay is where you write the connections down. Any prompt is abandonable the instant your own idea takes over.

Prompts — History of Life
  • What does “deep time” do to your sense of human significance — humbling, freeing, or unsettling?
  • Pick one extinction event. What’s most surprising about what survived versus what didn’t?
  • If the continents hadn’t drifted, how different might life look? Use one concrete example.
Prompts — Physics & Space
  • Which physics concept most changed how you see everyday life? Trace exactly why.
  • Is the maths-free understanding you’ve built “real” understanding? Argue it both ways.
  • Sagan’s “pale blue dot” — does the cosmic perspective console or diminish? Make your case.

Keep it lit-essay in spirit: a claim, evidence, and the connections you missed making — not a book report.
